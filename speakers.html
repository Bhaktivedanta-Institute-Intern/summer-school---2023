Prof. Martin Nowak

Harvard University, USA

Martin Nowak is presently a professor of mathematical biology at Harvard University since 2003 and Director of the Haravad’s Program for Evolutionary Dynamics. He is one of the leading researchers in the field that studies the role of cooperation in evolution. In 1989, he earned his doctorate in biochemistry and mathematics from the University of Vienna with thesis on quasispecies theory and evolution of cooperation. From 1989-1998, he was Erwin Schrodinger Postdoctoral Scholar at University of Oxford. He held professorships at University of Oxford and IAs, Princeton before moving to Harvard in 2003. His research interests include evolution of cooperation, evolution of language, evolutionary game theory and graph theory, somatic evolution of cancer, the dynamics of viruses and infectious diseases, pre-life and origins of life.
He is famous for his proposal and works in showing evolution requires third component called “cooperation” inaddition to the classical components of mutation and natural selection. Among his major discoveries are the mechanism of HIV disease progression, the first mathematical approach for studying the evolution of human language, the first quantification of the in vivo kinetics of human cancer and the five rules for the evolution of cooperation. He has published over 300 papers in world’s leading scientific journals including several in Nature and Science. He is a highly cited researcher with an h-index of 159. He authored many well-received books including Virus Dynamics (2000); Evolutionary Dynamics (2006); Super Cooperators (2011) and Evolution, Games & God (2013). He is a corresponding member of the Austrian Academy of Sciences. For his outstanding works, he received several international recognitions including Oxford’s Weldon Memorial Prize; the Albert Wander Prize of University of Bern; the Akira Okabo Prize of the Society for Mathematical Biology; the David Starr Jordan Prize jointly awarded by Oxford, Cornell and Indiana Universities and the Henry Dale Prize of the Royal Institution of London.
